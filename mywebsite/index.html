The Ethiopian lion, also known as the "Lion of Judah," is a prominent symbol in the Ethiopian flag. The lion is depicted in a regal pose, with its mane flowing and its paw resting on a ribbon that "Ethiopia" in Amharic, the country's official language. The Lion of Judah has deep cultural and historical significance in Ethiopia. According to Ethiopian tradition, the royal family of Ethiopia is believed to have descended from the Biblical King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. The Lion of Judah was also believed to have been one of the ancestors of the Ethiopian royal family, and was seen as a symbol of strength, courage, and royalty. The lion was used as a symbol of Ethiopian monarchy for centuries, and was prominently featured on the imperial flag during the reign of Emperor Haile Selassie, who ruled Ethiopia from 1930 until he was overthrown in a coup in 1974. The current Ethiopian flag, which was adopted in 1996, features a simplified version of the lion emblem as a nod to the country's history and traditions. Today, the Ethiopian lion continues to be an important symbol of national pride and unity, and is widely recognized as a symbol of Ethiopia around the world.

In Ethiopian culture, food plays an important role in hospitality and social interaction. Ethiopians are known for their warm and welcoming nature, and often express this through the act of sharing food with others. It is not uncommon for Ethiopians to invite guests into their homes and offer them injera and other traditional dishes as a sign of hospitality and respect.
